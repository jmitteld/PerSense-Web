Present or Future Value: the Concept
The promise of one dollar in 1999 has a certain value today. It is certainly less than one dollar. If you trust the promise, the present value is just the amount f money you would have to invest today in order for it to grow to one dollar in 1999.

Conversely, a dollar received in 1970 has a value of more than one dollar today. Its present value is simply the amount that it would have grown into today, had it been invested with compound interest.

The concept of present value may be broadly extended to include the value of any scheduled cash flow. If the cash flow was in the past, think of the present value as the balance of a hypothetical bank account in which each payment was deposited at its appointed time.

If the cash flow is in the future, think of a bank account with a certain present balance. How big must that balance be for you to withdraw each future payment at its appointed time, emptying the account with the last withdrawal? That is the present value of future payments.

You can also think of a present value as a loan: How big a loan can I take out today if I want to pay it back with this cash flow? This is an equivalent definition of present value of a future cash flow.

The future value of a cash flow is simply the "bank balance" on a given date in the future after a specified number of scheduled deposits have been made.

Future value can also be used to adjust a number of present dollars for inflation: put the inflation "rate" in the Yield column and the future date in the As of column.
