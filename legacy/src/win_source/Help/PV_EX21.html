Example 21: Interest on back taxes
Your ammended Federal tax return for 1990 shows an extra $1,000 that was not paid at that time. How much do you owe, with interest, on June 1, 1994?
The IRS specifies a table of rates that must be used to compute back taxes. For your convenience, PerSense Pro includes these rates as a template file which you can read in, just like any other saved screen, by pressing Ctrl-R for recall and selecting TAX-DUE. (A similar template of rates for tax refunds is called TAXREFND.) After recalling this file, enter the missed tax payment with its due date in the upper left, and today's date in the lower right. Notice the Computational settings appropriate to tax interest: 365 and DAY. Press C and read the amount due with interest from the Total value column at lower right.

Note 1: Rates quoted by the IRS are for daily compounding. You should set the Computational Setting for Basis days to 365 and for Default number of payments per year to DAY; then enter the IRS rates in the Daily Rates column. Alternatively, you can enter them as True Rates, and the slight difference in compounding will produce an answer that differs only by pennies. Note 2: There is not enough room in the portion of the rates table that is visible on screen for all the rates that we have displayed here. The window will scroll as you reach the bottom line, making room for up to 127 rates. Note 3: The table begins with rates that were in effect in 1985, although the tax deficiency did not start accruing interest until April, 1990. The extra rates on the screen are harmless, and ignored by PerSense.
