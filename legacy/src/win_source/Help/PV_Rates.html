True Rate, Loan Rate, and Yield
Interest rates are quoted differently in loan situations than in savings situations. An eight-percent savings account actually pays a little more interest han you pay on an eight-percent loan, because of compounding within each month. Remember: use the True Rate column for present value and passbook savings computation, the Loan Rate column for loan computations.

Occasionally, it is the yield rather than true rate or loan rate that is the interesting number. A yield is the actual amount of interest paid on one dollar during one year, after all compounding. Savings banks sometimes quote their yields alongside of rates, because the yield looks higher.

Bond yields and Canadian Rates are actually loan rates for semi-annual compounding period. You can set the compounding period used to compute loan rates from true rates (and true rates from loan rates) on the Computational Settings screen. The setting is labeled Default number of payments per year.

Similarly, a daily rate is actually a loan rate where interest is compounded from day to day. Numerically, this is almost identical to the true rate, which is for "moment to moment" compounding. If it is daily rates that you need to calculate, and the small difference between daily rate and true rate is important to you, you can set the Default number of payments in Computational Settings to DAY.

Note that true rate, loan rate, and yield are equivalent ways of specifying the same quantity, akin to the difference between measuring weight in pounds and in kilograms. Just as a weight of 150 pounds has exactly the same meaning as a weight of 68.2 kilograms, a true rate of 8.0% is exactly equivalent to a monthly loan rate of 8.0267% or a yield of 8.3287%.

The True Rate, Loan Rate, and Yield columns are linked in lock step. When you enter a number in any of them, the other two are computed mmediately.
