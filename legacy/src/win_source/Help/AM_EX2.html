Example 2: Computing APR with points
In the loan from Example 1, suppose the bank charges 2.5 points. What is the APR?
As a lender in the US, you are obligated by law to provide standard rate information to the borrower in the form of an APR, or Annual Percentage Rate. For loans in which the interest rate does not change and no points are charged, the APR is within a hair's breadth of the Loan Rate. If the loan date is exactly one month before the first payment, the APR is identical to the Loan Rate. If the loan date is earlier, the APR is very slightly higher than the Loan Rate if interest is prepaid, slightly lower if interest from the first partial period is amortized.

If points are levied, the APR becomes an interesting number, because it differs significantly from the Loan Rate, and the APR is a good measure of the true cost of borrowing.

The Amortization screen computes an APR if you fill in the column labeled Pts. (Points can be zero and APR will still be computed.)

Points cause the APR to be somewhat higher than the 12.4% loan rate. Even though the loan is paid over 30 years, the extra 2.5% paid as points is an important contribution to the cost of borrowing because it is taken "off the top" on the date the loan closes.
