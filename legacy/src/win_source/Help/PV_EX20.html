Example 20: Targeting a rate of return on a leveraged investment
You buy a small apartment building on 8/1/94 for $250,000 with a 10% downpayment, two points, and 7.5%, 30-year financing. You plan to sell it in six years. The estimated rental income is $2500/month with projected annual increases of 3%. Taxes, insurance, and maintenance costs average 00/month. How much must you sell it for to realize a 14% annualized return on your investment?
This is a complex but realistic computation, best accomplished on the Present Value screen after using the Amortization screen and the Mortgage screen to generate input numbers. The general rule for computing return on investment is to put the amount and date of the original investment in the top line of the Present Value block at the bottom of the screen. The initial investment amount could be computed by hand, using the points and downpayment, but here we will do it on the Mortgage screen. The other number we will need is the mortgage balance after six years. This is most easily computed on the Amortization screen.

First, to the Mortgage screen. Press Alt-M . Enter the downpayment percentage, the price, and the interest rate. Read your initial investment from the Cash Required column, and your monthly payment from the cell labeled Monthly Total.

The base investment on which you seek a 14% return is the Cash Required amount, $29,500, and the monthly costs are estimated at $2,273.20.

Now switch to the Amortization screen by pressing Alt-A . We need to compute the remaining balance of the loan, which will become due and payable when the property is sold in six years. Enter the loan data as in the Mortgage screen and use the As of block to compute the balance in six years:

The amount due in six years will be $211,448.15. We now have computed all the numbers we need for the target computation on the Present Value screen. Return to this screen with Alt-P , and set up the problem as follows:

Your initial investment is the cash required from the Mortgage screen. Enter this amount and date in the Present Value block at the bottom of the screen, and insert your target rate of return, 14%.

There are two sets of Periodic Payments associated with this investment: the monthly rental represents a positive contribution of $2,500 / month, escalating at 3% per year. Enter this on the top line. Balanced against this is the cost of the debt service, taxes, insurance, and maintenance, estimated at $2,273.23 on the Mortgage screen. Enter this figure on the second line, with a minus sign.

When the house is sold in 2000, the mortgage must be paid off. This is a negative component of your return, entered on the top line of the Single Payments block. The target proceeds from the house sale is the unknown in this calculation; enter just the date of expected sale as a second Single Payments line, and leave the amount to be computed.

Real estate investments are highly leveraged, and the numbers in this example are sensitive to your assumptions. You may find it interesting to change the $2500/month figure or the 3% COLA and see how they affect the target sale price.
