APRs and Loan Comparisons
APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate, and it is a standard measure of the true cost of borrowing, taking both points and rate into account. The definition of APR is in the Federal Truth In Lending Act.

When no points are charged, the APR is the same as the monthly loan rate as quoted in PerSense. When points are taken into account, the APR rises from this value.

The quoted APR for a loan is the full-term APR, the APR that corresponds to holding the loan through its full scheduled duration. If points are part of a loan and if you pay off the loan early, then the points will have a greater effect on the APR the earlier the loan is retired. To take an extreme case, a 30-year, 8% loan with 3 points has a nominal APR of 8.3238%. But if your plans change and you move after one year, you will have paid the entire 3 points plus 8% interest for a loan of a single year, and the APR is more than 11%!

In general, you can think of APR for any loan as a function of time. It starts very high, then declines steadily to the quoted value if the loan is held fully to term. Two different loans can be represented by two different lines. In general, one line will be lower (better APR) if the time is short, but then the lines cross and the other loan will have a better APR for longer times.

To compare APRs for two mortgages, PerSense computes APR as a function of how long you will hold the mortgage. If the APR lines for the two loans cross at some point in time, this is the duration that is reported to you in the criterion for choice. PerSense will say "If mortgage is held for more than N years, then mortgage A is better." But if the two lines do not cross, then one of the mortgages is unambiguously better than the other, and PerSense will tell you so.

If you are curious about how APR is actually computed, it is not so difficult to understand as you might think. See Example 9 from Chapter 9 of this manual, the Present Value chapter.
