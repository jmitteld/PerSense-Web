Example 1: Simple amortization table
Compute payments and print an amortization schedule for a $250,000 mortgage, amortized over 30 years at 12.4 percent, and closing on June 21, 1994.
Enter the loan amount (Amount borrowed), the closing date (Loan Date), and the interest rate (Loan rate %) in the first three cells. PerSense guesses that the 1st Pmt Date will be 8/1/94. Enter 360 periods, at the default 12 payments per year.

Typically, repayment of a loan that closes in mid-month begins on the first day of the second month following; in this case, payments will start August 1st, and interest will be prepaid at settlement for the last nine days of June. (The Computational setting for 1st interest prepaid at settlement should be set to YES for this kind of computation.)

Once you've entered the data, press Ctrl-T to print a table. Use the default settings DETAIL ONLY and Destination: SCREEN for now. Press F10 to begin. The table appears one screenful at a time.

The Date of each payment is listed at left. (A payment is listed on the settlement date whenever prepaid interest or points are payable.)

The Principal New Balance column holds the principal balance as it stands immediately after the day's payment.

The Interest This Period and Principal This Period columns always add up to the payment amount (here, $2,648.76), which remains constant (but is divided differently) for each payment. PerSense will not restate the payment on every line when it remains through the life of the loan.

The column labeled Interest is computed as the quoted loan rate divided by 12, multiplied by the principal balance the month before. For example, the second interest number on the chart above is $2583.33 [(12.4%/12) x 250,000]. The 250,000 comes directly from the Principal New Balance column as of the period before. This is appropriate to Interest in arrears (ARR), that is, interest charged on the preceding period. If you select Interest in Advance (ADV) from the Computational Settings screen, the corresponding number will be a product of the loan rate for one month times the Principal New Balance on the same line of the chart.

The rightmost column contains Interest To Date, a running total of the column immediately to its left.

Notice the pattern typical of most amortization tables: The payments are overwhelmingly interest in the early years, with more and more of the payment going to principal as time goes on. The last payment is almost all principal, and the final balance is zero.
