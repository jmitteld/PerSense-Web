Example 15: Computing a bond yield
On 3/12/95, you purchase a standard corporate bond maturing 1/1/04 with a coupon of 12%. You pay $124.50 for the bond. What is the yield to maturity?
This computation is more involved than you might think, because the rules governing bond interest are in a class by themselves. Although this is essentially a present value computation with an unknown rate, two peculiarities must be considered: First, bond yields are conventionally quoted as 2-payment loan rates, (the same way that rates are quoted for Canadian mortgages.) Second, you must pay "accrued interest" in addition to the stated purchase price.

Enter the date of purchase (3/12/95) and the bond price ($124.50) in the bottom block, with an unknown rate.

Enter the payment schedule above: 7/1/93 through 1/1/04; twice a year; $6 each payment.

Note: Corporate bonds pay interest twice a year, on the anniversary of the maturity date, and half a year earlier. The 360-day calendar is always used. The amount of the payment is just half the quoted "coupon," since there are two payments per year.

In the Single Payments block, enter the maturity date (1/1/04) and face amount of the bond (the returned principal is $100).

By convention, the quoted bond price is supplemented by an "accrued interest" payment. The bond coupon for the current period is pro-rated according to the sale date, and the portion that represents interest since the last interest payment is remitted, along with the quoted sale price, to the seller. In order to compute yield to maturity accurately, you must do a computation "on the side" of accrued interest. This may be done as follows:

From 1/1/95 to 3/12/95 is two months and 11 days. In the 360-day calendar, this is treated as 71 days (2 x 30 + 11). The 71 days is taken as a proportion of a 360-day year, and that proportion is applied to the $12 coupon to determine accrued interest: accrued interest = 71 / 360 x 12 = 2.37. Do this on the pop-up calculator: press Alt-K to enter the calculator. Type 71 / 360 * 12 =. Enter the result in memory by typing M and recall ( R ) it later in the Single Payments column. Accrued interest is addeddirectly to your cost for the bond. You can add $124.50 to $2.37 and substitute this number in the Present Value block, or, as we have done in this example screen, enter the accrued interest as a negative single payment (-2.37) on the purchase date (3/12/95).

Adjust the Computational Settings screen. Press Alt-S and select Computational Settings. Set Basis days per year to 360 and Default number of payments per year to 2. As noted above, it is conventional to quote bond yields as semi-annual yields, or loan rates with two payments per year.

Press C and read the yield to maturity from the Loan Rate column (8.0558%).
