Example 17: Present value of lost earnings
You are an attorney who specializes in personal injury cases. A client lost his eyesight in an accident in February 1993. His medical bills in that year totaled $85,000, with an additional $18,000 in 1994. He is 45 years old now, and expects he will never be able to work again. His weekly salary had been $610. at is the present value of his tangible losses?
Whether computations are done by a financial expert or anyone else, they are still subject to the same uncertainties: What will be the average interest rate over the next 20 years? How would your client's salary have changed over that time had he been able to work? With PerSense, you can explore these uncertainties and judge their effect on your computed answer.

Set up the Present Value screen as shown in the figure. The From date represents the first missed paycheck, and the Through date represents his projected retirement at age 65. The Single Payments column contains the two medical bills, and the value is computed as of the present.

Use the COLA column to indicate that his salary might have increased at a 3% annual rate over the course of his career.

Finally, test the sensitivity of the present value to different interest rates. In the Present Value block, enter today's date on all three lines in the As of column, and interest rates of 7, 7.5 and 8%.

Press C . Note the less than 7% difference in the present values when the lowest and highest rates are used. Apparently, your computation is not too sensitive to your assumed rate.

At other times, you might find there is a slight discrepancy. The discrepancy will appear if payments are monthly (or quarterly, etc) and the 365-day calendar is used, but not if payments are weekly or bi-weekly. In the real, 365-day calendar, months are of unequal length, and PerSense neglects this in order to calculate more quickly. By assuming that payments are spaced equally, the program is able to use a formula to compute the value of a whole set of payments at once, instead of summing the values individually. In these cases, the answer at the bottom of the table is slightly more accurate than the answer on the screen. If you want the screen as well as the table to reflect this extra accuracy you can choose the Exact setting in the Computational Settings screen. Press Alt-S and select the Computational Settings screen, then specify YES for Exact mode. (You should also specify 365- day basis; if you are computing in the 360-day calendar, you have already made the approximation of equal months.)
